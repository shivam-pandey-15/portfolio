# Chapter 7.4: Fine-Tuning Strategies

## Executive Summary

Once you have useful training pairs, the next question is not whether to train, but how. There is no single best fine-tuning strategy for every search system. The right choice depends on model size, hardware budget, latency constraints, storage cost, and how aggressively your domain differs from the base model's original training distribution.

In practice, most teams choose among four broad paths:

1. Full fine-tuning of all parameters.
2. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning such as LoRA.
3. Retrieval-specific representation shaping such as Matryoshka training.
4. Distillation from a stronger teacher into a smaller production model.

These strategies are not mutually exclusive. Mature systems often combine them. For example, a team may LoRA-tune a strong base model, train with Matryoshka truncation objectives, and distill from a cross-encoder reranker. The purpose of this chapter is to make those options legible and to explain which trade-offs matter in production.

---

## The Strategic Question

### What Are You Optimizing For?

A training strategy is a compromise among competing goals:

- retrieval quality
- GPU memory use
- training speed
- serving cost
- deployment simplicity
- future adaptability

If you optimize only for the highest possible offline metric, you may end up with a model that is expensive to serve or impossible to re-embed frequently. If you optimize only for cheap deployment, you may leave meaningful retrieval gains on the table.

That is why fine-tuning strategy should be chosen as part of system design, not as a purely ML decision.

### The Common Mistake

Many teams jump straight to full fine-tuning because it feels "most powerful." But the best strategy is often the one that achieves 95 to 99 percent of the quality gain at a fraction of the cost and risk. For embedding systems, maintainability matters almost as much as raw benchmark quality.

---

## Full Fine-Tuning

### What It Means

Full fine-tuning updates every trainable parameter in the base model. The optimizer maintains state for the entire network, gradients flow through every layer, and the representation space is allowed to shift as much as the data demands.

```python
for param in model.parameters():
    param.requires_grad = True
```

### Why Teams Choose It

Full fine-tuning gives the model the most freedom to adapt. It is often the best option when:

- the domain is very different from the base model's pretraining data
- the dataset is large and high quality
- the base model is still small enough to fit comfortably on your hardware
- you care about extracting every last bit of domain adaptation

### Strengths

| Strength | Why It Matters |
|----------|----------------|
| Maximum flexibility | Every layer can adapt to domain-specific concepts |
| Strongest ceiling | Often best peak quality when data is sufficient |
| Simpler conceptual model | No adapter-merging or side-path complexity |

### Weaknesses

| Weakness | Operational Cost |
|----------|------------------|
| High VRAM use | Optimizer states and gradients are large |
| More instability | Easier to overfit or forget general knowledge |
| Larger experiment cost | Each run is more expensive |

### When It Makes Sense

For a 100M to 300M parameter embedding model, full fine-tuning can be entirely reasonable if you have 24GB to 80GB class GPUs and a dataset that justifies it. For multi-billion-parameter models, it quickly becomes impractical for most teams.

## Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (LoRA and Friends)

### The Core Idea

Parameter-efficient fine-tuning keeps the original model weights frozen and trains a small set of new parameters that modify the model's behavior. LoRA is the most common approach. Instead of updating a large weight matrix directly, it learns a low-rank update.

Conceptually:

```python
W_effective = W_frozen + A @ B
```

Where:

- `W_frozen` is the original model weight matrix
- `A` and `B` are small trainable matrices
- the rank of the update is much smaller than the full matrix size

### Why LoRA Became Popular

LoRA gives most teams access to useful adaptation without the cost of full training. You can train larger models on smaller hardware, iterate faster, and store only the adapter weights.

Benefits:

- lower VRAM
- cheaper experimentation
- easier checkpoint storage
- faster domain iteration

### Practical LoRA Knobs

| Parameter | Typical Values | Effect |
|-----------|----------------|--------|
| Rank `r` | 8, 16, 32, 64 | Higher rank gives more expressive updates |
| Alpha | 16, 32, 64 | Scales the adapter contribution |
| Dropout | 0 to 0.1 | Regularizes the adapter |
| Target modules | `q_proj`, `v_proj`, sometimes more | Decides where adaptation happens |

### Where LoRA Can Fall Short

LoRA is powerful, but it is not free magic. It can underperform full fine-tuning when:

- the domain shift is extreme
- adaptation is needed across many layers and pathways
- the selected rank is too small
- the target modules are poorly chosen

That said, for many enterprise retrieval systems, LoRA gets surprisingly close to full fine-tuning while being much easier to operate.

## Matryoshka Representation Learning

### What Problem It Solves

Embedding size is expensive. Larger vectors increase:

- vector database storage
- network transfer costs
- ANN memory pressure
- cache size
- latency for similarity search at scale

Standard embeddings spread information across all dimensions. If you truncate them aggressively, quality can collapse. Matryoshka Representation Learning trains the model so that useful information is concentrated in the first dimensions, allowing truncation with much smaller quality loss.

### Intuition

A 768-dimensional embedding normally assumes all 768 dimensions are available. Matryoshka training asks the model to perform well not just at 768 dimensions, but also at smaller prefixes such as 512, 256, and 128.

```python
def matryoshka_loss(q, d, dims, loss_fn):
    total = 0.0
    for dim in dims:
        total += loss_fn(q[:, :dim], d[:, :dim])
    return total
```

### Why This Is Valuable in Production

It creates one model that supports multiple operating points:

- full dimension for offline evaluation
- medium dimension for cost-balanced retrieval
- low dimension for ultra-large corpora or edge deployments

That flexibility is especially valuable when different indexes or customers have different cost envelopes.

### The Trade-Off

Training becomes more complex and somewhat heavier because the loss is computed multiple times. But the production savings can be substantial if vector storage is a major part of system cost.

## Distillation Strategies

### Teacher-Student Training

A common production pattern is to use a strong but expensive teacher to supervise a smaller dense retriever. Teachers can include:

- cross-encoders
- larger embedding models
- rerankers trained on richer labels
- ensembles of lexical and semantic systems

The student model learns to approximate the teacher's preferences while preserving the deployment shape of a fast bi-encoder.

### Why Distillation Matters

The best ranking model is often too slow for first-stage retrieval. Distillation bridges that gap. It lets you borrow judgment from a richer model and transfer some of that structure into a cheaper embedding space.

This is especially helpful when you already have:

- a high-performing reranker
- manually curated top-result judgments
- limited capacity to serve large models online

### Typical Distillation Signals

- pairwise margins between positive and negative docs
- soft similarity scores
- ranking order over candidate sets
- hard negative selection from teacher outputs

## Choosing a Base Model Before Fine-Tuning

### Start Strong

Most teams should never train from scratch. The practical question is which pretrained embedding model is the best starting point.

Selection criteria:

- language coverage
- model size
- open-weight availability
- retrieval benchmark history
- tokenizer behavior on your domain text
- serving friendliness

### Examples of Sensible Choices

| Scenario | Typical Starting Point |
|----------|------------------------|
| English-only enterprise retrieval | BGE-base or E5-base class models |
| Multilingual retrieval | multilingual E5 or similar |
| Ultra-low-latency systems | MiniLM-class models |
| Distill-then-deploy workflows | larger teacher plus smaller student |

The best base model is rarely the one with the biggest general benchmark score. It is the one whose quality, latency, and tuning flexibility fit your stack.

## Hyperparameter Strategy

### The Most Important Levers

Across strategies, the same handful of levers dominate outcomes:

- learning rate
- effective batch size
- negative quality
- warmup schedule
- number of epochs
- max sequence length

### Why Retrieval Training Is Sensitive

Embedding fine-tuning changes geometry globally. A bad learning rate can distort the space quickly. Too many epochs can overfit head queries. Too little negative difficulty can produce a model that looks fine on easy cases but fails on real confusions.

A practical rule is to make one hard thing strong at a time:

1. stabilize data
2. stabilize loss
3. add hard negatives
4. optimize serving dimensions

## Common Strategy Combinations

### 1. Full Fine-Tune on a Small Base Model

Good for teams with moderate hardware and strong labels. This usually delivers excellent adaptation with minimal architectural complexity.

### 2. LoRA on a Medium or Large Base Model

Good for teams that want rapid iteration, adapter portability, and lower compute cost.

### 3. LoRA Plus Matryoshka

Good for large-scale retrieval where vector storage and ANN cost matter almost as much as model quality.

### 4. Distill From Cross-Encoder Into Bi-Encoder

Good when reranking quality is already strong and you want better first-stage retrieval.

These combinations are often more important than the individual technique names. The real question is what system constraint each combination addresses.

---

## Failure Modes and Trade-Offs

### Catastrophic Forgetting

Aggressive fine-tuning can destroy general language structure. The model becomes very good on internal jargon and noticeably worse on ordinary semantic relationships. Mixing in some general-domain retrieval data can help preserve broader behavior.

### Adapter Lock-In

PEFT approaches make experimentation easy, but organizations can accidentally accumulate many adapters with weak governance. Without disciplined versioning, it becomes unclear which adapter belongs to which index, benchmark, or domain.

### Truncation Without Validation

Matryoshka-style benefits only matter if you actually validate the lower-dimensional variants. Teams sometimes assume a 768-dimensional model can be safely cut to 128 because the training method sounds compression-friendly. That assumption must be benchmarked, not hoped for.

### Serving Mismatch

A model that is easy to train can still be awkward to deploy. Adapter merging, tokenizer drift, or inconsistent normalization across services can erase the gains from a carefully tuned training run.

## Practical Decision Table

| Constraint | Likely Best Strategy |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Strong GPUs, small model, maximum lift needed | Full fine-tuning |
| Limited VRAM, larger model, fast iteration | LoRA |
| Vector storage cost is a major concern | Matryoshka training |
| Strong teacher exists, online budget is tight | Distillation |
| Need a simple and reliable baseline | Full tune or LoRA on a proven small model |

This table is deliberately simple. The goal is to orient the decision, not replace benchmarking.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Fine-tuning strategy is a systems decision, not just an ML choice; it must balance retrieval quality, hardware cost, deployment simplicity, and storage impact.
2. Full fine-tuning offers the highest adaptation ceiling but costs more VRAM, experimentation time, and operational care.
3. LoRA and related PEFT methods often capture most of the quality gain with much lower training cost and easier iteration.
4. Matryoshka training is valuable when vector dimensionality itself is a production cost lever.
5. Distillation is a powerful bridge between slow, accurate teachers and fast, production-friendly dense retrievers.
