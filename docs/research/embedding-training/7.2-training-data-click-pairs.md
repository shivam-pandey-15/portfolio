# Chapter 7.2: Training Data, Click Pairs, and Hard Negatives

## Executive Summary

For embedding training, data quality matters more than model cleverness. A mediocre training recipe with high-quality domain pairs usually beats an elegant loss function trained on noisy signals. In production search, the most valuable supervision often comes from user behavior: what they searched, what they clicked, what they ignored, how long they stayed, and whether the session ended successfully.

That does not mean raw click logs are ground truth. Clicks are shaped by rank position, UI bias, freshness, brand familiarity, and even accidental taps. The central job of chapter 7.2 is to explain how to turn messy behavioral exhaust into training examples that teach the model something useful rather than simply recreating old ranking mistakes.

The practical output of this chapter is a mental pipeline:

1. Gather interaction logs.
2. Convert them into query-positive pairs.
3. Mine useful negatives.
4. Remove leakage and bias.
5. Build a train/dev/test split that reflects real search behavior.

---

## Why Training Data Dominates Outcomes

### The Model Learns Whatever Your Pairs Imply

An embedding model is not given direct definitions of relevance. It infers relevance from examples. If your positives are weak, your embedding space becomes fuzzy. If your negatives are too easy, the model learns only coarse separation. If your dataset is biased, the model faithfully reproduces that bias.

This is why two teams can start from the same base model and get completely different outcomes:

- Team A uses raw clicks with no filtering and random negatives.
- Team B uses cleaned sessions, dwell thresholds, hard negatives, and leakage controls.

Team B is not necessarily using a better architecture. They are giving the model a better map of what "good retrieval" means.

### Search Logs Are Valuable Because They Encode Intent

Behavioral data is powerful because it links a query to a decision. A user typed something, saw a set of results, chose one item, maybe spent time with it, maybe converted, maybe reformulated the query. That sequence contains much richer supervision than isolated text similarity.

Examples:

- Query: "cancel invoice in bulk"
- Clicked doc: "Batch invoice void workflow"
- Long dwell time: strong signal the doc helped
- No reformulation afterward: even stronger signal

The user never explicitly labeled the doc as "relevant," but their behavior strongly suggests it.

## Types of Training Signal

### Explicit Labels

These come from human annotation or direct user feedback:

- Relevance judgments
- Thumbs up / down
- Quality review from subject matter experts
- Labeled duplicate questions

Advantages:

- High precision
- Easier to interpret
- Better for evaluation as well as training

Disadvantages:

- Expensive
- Slow to scale
- Often too small for robust fine-tuning

### Implicit Feedback

This is the dominant production signal:

- Result clicks
- Dwell time
- Add-to-cart
- Ticket resolution
- Purchase after search
- Copy/download/open actions
- Session abandonment or reformulation

Advantages:

- Large volume
- Continuously refreshed
- Tied to real user intent

Disadvantages:

- Noisy
- Biased by ranking and presentation
- Requires interpretation rather than blind usage

### Synthetic Data

When real interactions are scarce, teams often generate pseudo-queries from documents using LLMs or internal experts. This is useful for cold start systems, but synthetic data usually works best as a bootstrap layer rather than a complete substitute for real behavior.

## Turning Logs Into Query-Positive Pairs

### Start With Sessions, Not Isolated Events

A single click event is too thin. Good pipelines reconstruct sessions:

- The original query
- The ranked list shown
- The clicked result
- Position of the result
- Time to click
- Dwell time
- Follow-up query or abandonment
- Final success event, if available

This lets you distinguish useful clicks from weak ones.

### Heuristics for Positive Pair Construction

A clicked document becomes a positive when several signals align:

- Reasonable dwell time
- No immediate bounce
- No instant reformulation
- Session ended successfully
- Position did not make the click inevitable

A common production heuristic looks like this:

| Signal | Interpretation |
|--------|----------------|
| Click + long dwell | Strong positive |
| Click + conversion | Very strong positive |
| Click + short dwell + reformulation | Weak or noisy positive |
| No click after many impressions | Potential negative evidence |

### Example Pipeline

```python
def build_positive_pairs(session_logs, min_dwell=20):
    pairs = []

    for session in session_logs:
        if not session.query:
            continue

        for click in session.clicks:
            if click.dwell_seconds < min_dwell:
                continue

            if session.reformulated_within_seconds(10):
                continue

            pairs.append({
                "query": session.query,
                "doc_id": click.doc_id,
                "strength": "strong_positive"
            })

    return pairs
```

The key idea is that "clicked" is not enough. You want "clicked and likely useful."

## The Biases Hidden in Click Data

### Position Bias

Users examine top results more than lower results. A rank-1 result gets clicked more often partly because it is better and partly because it is seen first. If you train naively on raw clicks, the model may just learn the previous system's rank distribution.

### Presentation Bias

A result with a bright thumbnail, a trusted brand, or a bolded keyword snippet can get more clicks even if it is less relevant. In e-commerce, image quality and price badges distort behavior. In knowledge search, document titles can dominate clicks even when document content is weak.

### Freshness Bias

Users may prefer newer content because it appears recent, not because it is semantically more relevant. This can leak freshness preference into the embedding space even when it should be handled later by ranking.

### Popularity Bias

Popular items absorb more clicks. If a model is trained only from clicks, it may collapse toward already popular entities and under-serve the long tail.

## Negative Sampling: Where Most Systems Either Win or Waste Their Training

### Why Random Negatives Are Not Enough

If the query is "reset mac vpn" and your negative is a cooking recipe, the model learns almost nothing. That negative was already far away in semantic space. Easy negatives are useful early, but they do not teach the fine boundaries that production search needs.

### Types of Negatives

| Negative Type | Description | Value |
|---------------|-------------|-------|
| Random negative | Arbitrary doc from corpus | Easy baseline signal |
| In-batch negative | Positive doc for another query in same batch | Efficient and common |
| Lexical hard negative | Shares many words but is wrong | Strong for fine-grained separation |
| Behavioral hard negative | Highly exposed, not clicked | Strong if debiased well |
| Model-mined hard negative | Retrieved by current dense model but judged wrong | Excellent for late-stage refinement |

### Hard Negatives Are What Teach Distinctions

Examples:

- Query: "iphone 15 charger"
- Positive: USB-C charging adapter for iPhone 15
- Hard negative: iPhone 15 protective case

- Query: "postgres replication lag"
- Positive: runbook for diagnosing replica lag
- Hard negative: documentation for replication setup

The hard negative is dangerous because it looks relevant. That is exactly why it is valuable. The model must learn the subtle difference.

### A Practical Negative Mining Strategy

1. Start with in-batch negatives.
2. Add BM25-mined negatives that overlap lexically.
3. Add top-k ANN negatives from the current embedding model.
4. Filter out likely false negatives.

This curriculum usually works better than jumping straight into only the hardest negatives.

## False Negatives: The Silent Model Killer

### What Makes Them Dangerous

A false negative is a document labeled or treated as irrelevant even though it is actually relevant. In search, this happens constantly because multiple documents can satisfy a query.

Example:

- Query: "benefits enrollment deadline"
- Positive doc from click logs: HR annual enrollment guide
- "Negative" doc: Benefits FAQ page with the same deadline

If you push that FAQ away from the query, you damage the embedding space.

### How Teams Reduce False Negatives

- Exclude docs from the same category or entity cluster as the positive.
- Remove duplicates and near-duplicates.
- Use teacher rerankers or LLM judges to filter uncertain negatives.
- Skip negatives whose lexical and semantic similarity are both very high.
- Prefer "shown but skipped above clicked result" over arbitrary corpus negatives.

A strong negative is one that is convincingly wrong, not merely unlabeled.

## Building the Dataset

### Recommended Record Shapes

Pair format:

```json
{"query": "reset sso token", "positive": "Runbook for forcing a new SSO token on macOS clients."}
```

Triplet format:

```json
{
  "query": "reset sso token",
  "positive": "Runbook for forcing a new SSO token on macOS clients.",
  "negative": "SSO admin policy overview and compliance settings."
}
```

Multi-positive format:

```json
{
  "query": "benefits enrollment deadline",
  "positives": [
    "Annual benefits enrollment guide for employees.",
    "Benefits FAQ including deadline and cutoff policy."
  ]
}
```

The best format depends on the loss function and tooling, but the conceptual goal is the same: encode what should be near and what should be far.

### Split by Query Family, Not Just Rows

One of the easiest ways to fool yourself is letting near-identical query variants leak across train and test:

- Train: "reset okta token"
- Test: "how do i reset okta token"

That is not real generalization. Good splits keep related query families together.

Recommended split principles:

- Group by normalized query intent.
- Split chronologically when possible.
- Keep head and tail queries represented in every split.
- Preserve domain slices for diagnostic evaluation.

## Bootstrap Strategies When You Lack Click Data

### Synthetic Queries

LLMs can generate plausible user queries from each document or chunk. This works well for:

- New products
- Private knowledge bases
- Low-traffic enterprise systems
- High-value domains with no usable click logs

But synthetic data tends to be too clean. Human queries are messy, abbreviated, misspelled, and task-driven. So synthetic query generation should include prompt diversity:

- short keyword queries
- natural-language questions
- error-message style queries
- synonym-rich variants
- novice and expert phrasing

### Expert-Labeled Seeds

Even 500 to 2,000 carefully labeled query-document examples can be extremely useful for evaluation and for early fine-tuning if paired with in-batch negatives.

### Weak Supervision From Existing Systems

You can also distill from:

- high-performing rerankers
- curated FAQ mappings
- support macros to article links
- manual collections or merchandised results

These are often underused data sources.

---

## Dataset Quality Checklist

Before training, ask:

1. Are positives actually useful, or merely clicked?
2. Are negatives hard enough to matter?
3. Have we filtered likely false negatives?
4. Is the dataset balanced across head and tail queries?
5. Are train/dev/test splits leakage-safe?
6. Does the dataset represent live production behavior?

If the answer to several of these is no, better training code will not save the project.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. High-quality training data is the main determinant of custom embedding performance; raw click logs are useful but never ground truth by themselves.
2. Good positive pairs come from session-aware behavioral evidence such as dwell time, lack of immediate reformulation, and successful session endings.
3. Hard negatives are essential because they teach the model fine semantic boundaries that easy negatives cannot.
4. False negatives can quietly damage the embedding space, so filtering and debiasing matter as much as data volume.
5. When click data is missing, synthetic queries, expert labels, and weak supervision can bootstrap a viable training set.
